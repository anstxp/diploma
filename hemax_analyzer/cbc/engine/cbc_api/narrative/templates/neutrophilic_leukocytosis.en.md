# Neutrophilic leukocytosis — sign of active inflammation
icon: 🔥
severity: medium
---
Your **WBC count is elevated mostly due to neutrophils** — a classic picture of **neutrophilic shift**. Most often this is the **body's response to inflammation or infection**, less often a stress reaction or a medication side effect.

**Common "everyday" sources** (usually the first to look for):
- **Respiratory tract** — cold, bronchitis, sinusitis, pneumonia
- **ENT** — otitis, tonsillitis, pharyngitis
- **Teeth/gums** — periodontitis, root abscess, stomatitis
- **Skin** — boils, infected wounds, cellulitis
- **Urinary tract** — cystitis, pyelonephritis
- **GI tract** — gastroenteritis, food poisoning

**Less common but more serious** (require physician assessment):
- Acute appendicitis, cholecystitis, diverticulitis
- Internal abscesses, septic foci
- Deep soft-tissue infections

**Non-infectious causes:**
- Stress response — after trauma, surgery, severe pain, intense exercise
- Corticosteroids, lithium, G-CSF — pharmacologically raise neutrophils

This is **not** a "minor deviation". Elevated WBC with neutrophil predominance is a clinically meaningful signal that requires a physician's assessment.
---
actions:
- **Think about symptoms in the last 1-2 weeks**: fever, pain (where?), cough, discharge, dysuria, wounds, bowel problems
- Order **C-reactive protein (CRP)** — rises quickly with bacterial infections; combined with neutrophilic leukocytosis, this is stronger evidence of active infection
- If local symptoms are present — additionally, by physician's order: urinalysis, urine/sputum culture, ultrasound of the relevant area
- **See your family doctor or GP soon** — to find the source
- With fever ≥38°C or worsening symptoms — **same-day** physician visit or ER
- Do not self-treat with antibiotics — proper choice requires knowing the etiology
- If you are taking corticosteroids, recently had surgery/trauma, or were under severe stress — this may produce a reactive rise without infection; discuss with your doctor
- Repeat CBC in 5-7 days — to assess the dynamics
---
red_flags:
- Temperature ≥38°C, especially with chills or sweating
- Severe localized pain (abdomen, back, chest, head) — **same-day** physician visit
- Confusion, drowsiness, rapid breathing — possible sepsis, **call emergency**
- Pale, mottled skin, cold extremities with fever — urgent physician visit
- Severe malaise, inability to stand — **EMERGENCY**
