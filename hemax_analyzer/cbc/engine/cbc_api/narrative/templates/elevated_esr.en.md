# Elevated ESR — sign of inflammation
icon: 🔥
severity: low
---
ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate) is a non-specific inflammation marker — the rate at which red blood cells settle in a tube over one hour. An elevated ESR means inflammation proteins are present in the blood, but it doesn't point to a specific cause.

Common causes of elevated ESR include:

- **Infections** — viral, bacterial (especially chronic)
- **Autoimmune diseases** — arthritis, vasculitis, lupus
- **Anemia** — low hemoglobin elevates ESR independently of inflammation
- **Pregnancy and older age** — physiological elevation
- Less common: malignancies, kidney disease, thyroiditis

ESR should be interpreted **together with C-reactive protein (CRP)** and clinical context — on its own, it has limited diagnostic value.
---
actions:
- Get **C-reactive protein (CRP)** test — a more specific inflammation marker
- If you have symptoms (fever, pain, fatigue) — see your primary care physician
- Check vital signs: temperature, blood pressure, pain
- If you have a chronic condition (arthritis, hepatitis, etc.) — discuss disease activity with your doctor
- Further tests depend on clinical findings: rheumatology panel, thyroid panel, tumor markers — as prescribed
---
red_flags:
- ESR > 50 mm/hr — urgent medical evaluation needed
- Persistent low-grade fever (37-38°C) for over a week
- Unexplained weight loss, night sweats
- Severe joint, muscle, or bone pain
- Lymph node enlargement
